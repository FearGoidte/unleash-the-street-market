Learn to talk like a market trader

Glossary of common market terms

Like any industry, markets and street trading have developed their own jargon. Some are special terms for things peculiar to market trading, others are slang. Start casually dropping the following lingo into you conversations to convince people that you were born on a barrow and grew-up on cobbled streets.

Most of these definitions are lifted from Gavin Kenning Engineering’s Market Trader Phrase Book.

Casual
a trader who does not have a regular pitch on a market and is assigned one on the day
Barrow
a wheeled cart used to display stock
Coster
Costermonger
someone who sells fruit, vegetables, fish, meat from a stall
to sell fruit, vegetables, fish, meat from a stall
Demmer
a trader who demonstrates their product and how it works (such as carpet cleaner) in order to make a sale
Gaff
a market
Grafter
a trader who works hard to sell his products, usually by talking or shouting about what they have to offer
Joint
a market stall
Kipper Season
the period of slow retail trade between January and March each year
Line
the type of product a trader sells
Lurker
a trader who observes their pitch from a distance, waiting until a customer shows interest in a product
Overhang
the roof section that extends over the front and/or rear legs of a stall to provide weather protection for customers, traders, and products
Pitch
the open space from which a trader sells their products
Pitcher
a trader who works from a raised platform, talking or shouting about the products they have on offer, often in an entertaining way
Punter
a customer or potential customer showing interest in buying a product
Regular
trader who works on a prearranged predictable pitch
Snide
counterfeit copies of a branded product
Spieler
a trader who tries to convince a customer in all manner of ways that a product is worth buying
Swag
cheap or small products, often referred to by wholesalers as Swag Lines
Toby
the rent for a pitch on a market
the person who collects the rent for a pitch

Back slang, the secret language of costermongers

Back slang originated in the 1830s as a means for costermongers to discretely discuss prices, their customers, and their other private affairs.

Over time it became a more associated with butchers and faded out of use amongst other traders.

The only word that made it from back slang into the wider English language is ‘yob’ which did not carry its modern negative characteristics and merely meant boy.
